2.03 Population Ecology

Population Ecology

  • Population - group of interacting organisms of the same species
    • Pre-reproductive juveniles and reproductive adults
  • Demographic structure: Numbers of individuals of each age or stage
  • Vary in size & density
  • Geographic range has bounds established by other species and physical limits
  • Dynamics of growth

Exponential (Geometric) Population Growth

  • Assume every individual produces 2 offspring then dies (double size each generation)
    • How an ideal bacterium in unlimited resources would reproduce
  • Growth rate is constant in population size N
    • intrinsic rate of natural increase (r) = birth rate (b) - death rate (d)
Exponential growth equation $\frac{dN}{dt}=rN$ helps model growth over time $t$. If size at next generation is current size times growth rate in that interval, we see stepwise growth. $N(t+1) = N(t)e^r$. $e^r$ is the discrete growth rate $\lambda$. $\lambda$ and $r$ are fixed, but population explodes in size quickly, quickly consuming all habitat & resources. $N_1 = N_0 + B - D + I - E$

Logistic Population Growth Levels Off at a Carrying Capacity

  • Carrying capacity: maximum pop size the environment can sustain
    • Individuals must be dying at similar rate to birth to keep same pop size
    • Growth rate slows as size increases, stops when carrying capacity is reached
$$\frac{dN}{dt}=rN\left(\frac{K-N}{K}\right)$$ where $K$ is carrying capacity

Population size is regulated by factors that are dependent or independent of population density

  • Biological factors: predation, competition, parasitism, mutualism, disease, etc
  • Non-biological factors: temperature, precipitation, pollution, salinity, pH, etc
    • All can change size, but only biological factors can regulate populations
    • Mutualism does not regulate becuase it promotes growth
  • Bio factors affect dense & sparse populations differently (disease for example)
    • Territoriality, disease, predation, toxic wastes

Life history traits and their evolution

  • Life cycle for each population is optimized, explained by Life History Theory
    • Optimized, not maximized, due to tradeoffs between survival & reproduction
  • Life history tradeoff as explained above, leading hypothesis is that energy is limiting factor

  • Life table is basic tool of demography
    • Used to study characteristics like survival, fecundity, mortality
    • Critical when ecologists want to know how well endangered or transplanted populations are doing
  • Cohort life tables follow one group born at the same time until death of all individuals
    • Key assumptions:
      • Populations ample of each age class is proportional to numbers in population
      • Age-specific mortality rates constant during time period
  • (table on website)
    • First row represents birth year, each subsequent row is one year later, not all survive year to year
    • Survivorship: mortality as a proportion of the original cohort
    • Age-specific fecundity: avg offspring born to individuals, estimated from data
  • If assumptions are met, sum of (survivorship * fecundity) at each age gives growth parameter R0
  • When R0 > 1, population is producing more offspring than losing from death (growing)
  • Patterns of age-specific survival predict general life history of population, creating survivorship curves (lx vs. time)
    • Type I: low mortality at young ages, high mortality as age increases
    • Type II: mortality constant, regardless of age
    • Type III: high mortality at early age, need high birth rates to sustain, but low mortality as age increases
