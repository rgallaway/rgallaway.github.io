1.04 Mechanisms of Evolution

4 key mechanisms of evolution: mutation, genetic drift, natural selection, and gene flow

Mutation generates variation

  • Mistake in DNA occurs in heritable cells
  • Single-celled asexual organisms: whole cell & DNA passed on in binary fission
  • Sexual organisms: mutations passed on if in egg/sperm cells
  • Small or neutral mutations more common since large mutations often result in death
  • Standing genetic variation - created through random mutation, must be present for evolution to occur
  • Mutation rates are low, ranging from 10-6 for human genes to 10-10 for bacterial genes
    • Means that mutation alone doesn’t have huge effect on evolution, but combination does

Populations are constantly under the influence of genetic drift

  • Alleles that make it to next gen of population are a random sample of those in the current population
    • Not every allele makes it through; some overrepresented while others decline
  • Subtle effect in large populations, overshadowed by selection or gene flow
    • Can dramatically affect small populations

Evolution by natural selection results in individuals that are a better fit to their environment

  • Pressure from environment causes only some phenotypes to survive & reproduce
    • Those with better fit for environment have higher Darwinian fitness
  • Galápagos finches are good example of natural selection
    • Drought reduced number of small seeds, so small-beaked birds starved
    • Average phenotype shifted to larger beaks, reduced genetic diversity

Gene flow (migration) makes two different populations more similar to each other

  • Different populations subject to different pressures, different allele frequencies
  • With migration, populations experience changes in allele frequencies, become more similar

Non-random mating results from mate choice

  • Real populations do not mate randomly
    • Evidence shows that animals choose mates with different HLA genotypes
  • Non-random mating with “like” individuals shifts frequencies in favor of homozygotes
    • “unlike” individuals shifts in favor of heterozygotes
  • Can occur without changing allele frequency

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium Principle

  • Baseline to measure change against
    • To calculate, assume no selection, mutation, drift, or gene flow, and random mating
  • Individuals are diploids (two copies of each gene)
    • Assume frequencies do not change between generations
