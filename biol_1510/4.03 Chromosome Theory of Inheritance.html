4.03 Chromosome Theory of Inheritance

Genes are Located on Chromosomes

  • Chromosome Theory of Inheritance - idea that genes are located on chromosomes
    • Proposed by Thomas Hunt Morgan using fruit flies
      • Normal flies have red eyes (dominant), Morgan discovered white eyed mutation
      • Eye color gene was located on X chromosome since males & females had different results
  • Sex Linkage: inheritance of genes on the sex chromosomes
    • Females have 2 copies of X chromosome, but males have one
      • Males with recessive allele will always show recessive trait
    • Most genes are on autosomes, of which both males & females have two copies
    • Principle of segregation & independent assortment are part of this theory of inheritance

  • Genetic linkage: genes controlling 2 different traits are located near each other on same chromosome
    • if two genes are on same chromosome, and you inherit the whole chromosome, you have to inherit both genes together
  • Crossing-over shuffles alleles for genes located on the same chromosome
    • Crossover results in genetic recombination
    • Occurs in prophase I, when homologues align and synapse, physically swaps genetic material between non-sister chromatids
    • Occurs randomly along chromosome, so the closer two genes are together, the less likely crossover will occur
      • Two genes on same chromosome will assort independently if they are far enough apart that crossover almost always occurs

Simple Rules for Pedigree Analysis

  • Autosomal Recessive
    • Affects males & females equally
    • Both parents must carry allele
    • Parents may not display trait (carriers)
    • ~1/4 of children affected (if both parents are carriers)
  • Autosomal Dominant
    • Affects males & females equally
    • Only one parent must carry allele
    • If child displays trait, at least one parent must also display trait
    • ~1/2 children affected (if one parent displays trait)
  • X-linked Recessive
    • Typically affects only males
    • Affected male passes allele to daughters, not sons
    • Trait skips a generation
