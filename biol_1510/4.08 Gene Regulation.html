4.08 Gene Regulation

  • Cells only express a subset of their genes
  • Respond & adapt to environment by toggling expression of certain genes
  • Cells in different parts of organisms differentiate by making different proteins
  • Most often regulated at transcription level
  • 3 levels of regulating gene expression:
    • Transcriptional control - whether/how much a gene is translated into mRNA
    • Translational control - whether/how much mRNA is translated into protein
    • Post-translational control - whether protein is active/inactive, stable/degraded
  • All 3 domains use:
    • Positive regulation (turning on gene expression)
    • Negative regulation (turning off gene expression)
    • Co-regulation (turning genes on/off together)
  • Differences in specifics between prokaryotes and eukaryotes

Commonalities: Promoters & Regulatory Elements

  • Promoters - sites where DNA/RNA polymerase bind to initiate transcription
    • Contain binding sites for transcription factors, which either attract or repel RNA polymerase
  • Regulatory Element - DNA sequence that transcription factors recognize & bind to
    • Promoter along with transcription factor binding elements regulate transcription
    • Can be used for either positive or negative control
    • Single gene can be subject to multiple levels of control
  • Some genes are constitutively expressed: always transcribed

Differences: Mechanisms of Co-Regulation

  • Both prokaryotic & eukaryotic cells can co-regulate genes, but have different mechanisms
  • Prokaryotes:
    • Co-regulated genes organized into an operon - 2 or more functionally-related genes are transcribed from single promoter into one long mRNA
    • Translated to make proteins encoded by genes in the operon
    • Start at 5’ end, start translating at first AUG, terminate at stop codon, start again at next AUG
  • Eukaryotes:
    • Do not have operons, instead co-regulated genes have same DNA regulatory element sequence
      • Same activator/repressor can regulate every single gene with that element, even on different chromosomes
    • Eukaryote-specific complexities: chromatin & alternative splicing
      • Eukaryotic DNA organized around histones which organize DNA into nucleosomes, together called chromatin
        • Condensed into formations where DNA between nucleosomes is more accessible, controlled by post-translational modifications added to histones
        • With acetyl groups added, nucleosomes are less tightly packed, allowing transcription factors access
        • With them removed, transcription factors cannot reach the DNA, so gene is repressed
      • Eukaryotic mRNA must be processed with 5’ cap, splicing of introns, and 3’ poly-A tail
        • Each step subject to regulation, along with export to cytoplasm and stability in cytoplasm
      • Eukaryotic genes have different splice variants - different exons included in different mRNAs transcribed from same gene (alternative splicing)
        • Different types of cells often express different splices of the same gene
