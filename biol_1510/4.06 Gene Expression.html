4.06 Gene Expression

The Central Dogma

  • Flow of information from nucleic acid to protein
  • DNA transcribed to RNA, RNA translated to sequence of amino acids

Transcription: DNA to RNA

  • Enzyme RNA polymerase reads template strand of DNA, synthesizes RNA molecule with complementary bases
  • RNA is synthesized 5’ -> 3’, RNA polymerase reads template DNA 3’ -> 5’
  • Sequence of bases is same as sequence in “coding” strand of DNA, except RNA has U instead of T
  • RNA polymerases in both prokaryotes & eukaryotes depend on transcription factors (DNA-binding proteins) to bind special sequence motifs in DNA called promoters
  • Transcription factors recruit RNA polymerase to bind to promoter sequence and begin transcription just downstream of promoter

Prokaryotes: Transcription and Translation are Coupled

  • Ribosomes begin to translate even while mRNA is still being transcribed

Eukaryotes: transcription & translation are separated in space & time, nuclear pre-mRNA undergoes processing to become mature mRNA

  • Transcription occurs in the nucleus, while translation occurs either by free cytoplasmic ribosomes or ribosomes docked to ER
  • RNA transcribed from protein-coding gene in nucleus is called pre-mRNA, has to undergo at least 2, usually 3 processing steps before exported as mature mRNA
    • 5’ end is modified by covalent attachment of 7-methylG nucleotide (5’ cap), required to initiate translation
    • For genes with introns, non-protein coding sequences (introns) are removed by RNA splicing, leaving exons
    • 3- end of pre-mRNA is modified by addition of hundreds of adenine nucleotides (polyA tail), important for nuclear export, mRNA stability, and translation
  • All happens during transcription, so full-length “pre-mRNA” never exists

Translation: RNA to Protein

  • Translating RNA bases to amino acid sequence requires 3 things: mRNA, ribosomes, and tRNAs charged with corresponding amino acids
  • mRNA transcribed from protein-coding genes
  • Ribosomes are assemblies of ribosomal RNA molecules and dozens of proteins
  • tRNAs match amino acid to codon in mRNA, anticodon loop has complementary bases to mRNA codon
    • 3’ end of tRNA has high-energy bond to appropriate amino acid
    • Cells have enzymes called amino-acyl tRNA synthases that charge tRNAs with correct amino acid
  • Translation begins near 5’ end of mRNA, typically at AUG triplet
    • Ribosome moves along 3 bases at a time, tRNA with complementary anticodons arrive with amino acids
    • Peptide bond forms, one end has free amino group (N-terminus) and other end has free carboxyl group (C-terminus) 
      • New amino acids only added to free carboxyl group, so grows from N-terminus to C-terminus

The Genetic Code

  • Used by all living organisms, only minor modifications in relatively few species
  • “Degenerate” because many amino acids specified by 2, 3, 4, or 6 different codons

Mutations can have vastly different effects based on where they occur

  • DNA base substitutions may have no effect if changing 3rd base of codon
    • Synonymous mutations
    • Exact change still matters, polarity can be disruptive, some parts of proteins are more important than others
  • Other substitutions in 1st or 2nd base cause amino acid changes, called “non-synonymous” or mis-sense
  • Some mutations cause new stop codon, called “nonsense” and cause truncated polypeptides
  • Insertions/deletions (indels) can cause change in all downstream codons, called “frameshift” mutations
