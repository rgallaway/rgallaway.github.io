3.05 Respiration, Chemiosmosis, and Oxidative Phosphorylation

ATP Synthesis

  • All cells use energy released from ATP hydrolysis
  • ATP is made either by substrate-level phosphorylation or oxidative phosphorylation of ADP
    • Substrate-level phosphorylation: phosphate transferred to ADP from high-energy phosphorylated organic compound.
      • Only small amounts made this way in cells undergoing respiration
    • Oxidative phosphorylation: bulk of cell’s ATP during respiration, proton-motive force across membrane provides energy for ATP synthase to make ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate
      • Proton-motive force is created by large difference in proton concentrations across membrane
      • All prokaryotes maintain proton (pH) gradient across plasma membranes, mitochondria generate proton gradient across inner mitochondrial membrane
      • Interior is relatively alkaline, exterior is relatively acidic
      • Force comes from combination of proton gradient and voltage gradient across membrane
        • Imbalance of protons creates difference in charge
      • Form of stored energy: protons returning down concentration gradient release free energy that can be captured to make ATP
      • Lipid bilayer is almost impermeable to protons

F1ATP Synthase

  • Located in membrane, rotor-stator molecular machine
  • Proton motor force drives protons through channel, turns rotor at 100 RPM, changing shape of cytoplasmic subunits (F1 ATPase), which bind ADP and inorganic phosphate to form ATP.
    • Each 360-degree turn results in synthesis of 3 ATP molecules
  • Synthases in mitochondira, chloroplasts, and bacteria are all structurally similar, homologous molecularly
    • More distant homology with archaeal synthases and vacuolar membrane ATPase
  • Can work in reverse to hydrolyze ATP and pump protons across membrane

Chemiosmosis creates proton gradient

  • Chemiosmosis: process of pumping protons across membrane to generate proton gradient
    • Driven by flow of electrons down electron transport chain made up of protein complexes that pass electrons down, pumping a proton across membrane for each electron it passes on
    • Last complex in transport chain passes electrons to O2 to make water (aerobic respiration)
  • Respiration - passage of electrons down the transport chain
    • We breathe oxygen because oxygen is terminal electron receptor, end of line for transport chain
    • Many bacteria can use other electron acceptors in anaerobic respiration
  • Transfer of electrons from one molecule to another is an oxidation-reduction (redox) reaction.
    • Oxidative phosphorylation gets name from redox reactions that occur in transport chain

Redox Reactions and NAD+/NADH

  • Molecule that loses electrons is oxidized, gains electrons is reduced
  • Different molecules have different tendencies to gain/lose electrons, called redox potential
    • Reaction between molecules with large difference in redox potential releases lots of free energy
    • In aqueous environments, electrons accompanied by protons, result is that hydrogen atoms are transferred
      • enzymes that carry out redox reactions are called dehydrogenases
  • Metabolism features series of redox reactions
    • Heterotrophs oxidize organic molecules, reduce an electron carrier molecule called NAD+
      • Accepts electrons to become NADH, cycles back to NAD+ by giving electrons to first complex of membrane electron transport chain
    • NAD+/NADH is key intermediary in shuttling electrons from food molecules to electron transport chain for respiration
    • Oxidation of NADH: NADH + H + O2 —> NAD+ + H2O
      • Highly exergonic, free energy change of -54 kcal/mol
    • ETC and chemiosmosis is strategy to maximize amount of ATP made from large amounts of free energy
      • Subdivides NADH oxidation to series of lower energy redox reactions, used to pump protons
      • H+ gradient across membrane is a form of stored energy

Anaerobic Respiration

  • Energy released by redox reactions, thus available for ATP synthesis, depends on redox potential of terminal electron receptor
  • O2 has greatest potential, but bacteria and archaea can use others in anaerobic respiration, producing less ATP
    • Bacteria switch to best available sources & sinks available in environment
    • Marine sediment microbial communities stratify according to redox potential
      • Deeper layers use acceptors with progressively lower reducing potential
  • Prokaryotes can use proton motive force for active transport across the plasma membrane, and to power motor complex to rotate flagellum
  • Mitochondrial & thylakoid membrane correspond to original plasma membranes of ancestral endosymbionts
  • Oxidative phosphorylation operates in both anaerobic and aerobic environments

Cellular Metabolic Pathways

  • Classify organisms according to source of energy & organic carbon:
    • heterotrophs: get energy & carbon from metabolism of pre-existing organic compounds
    • photoautotrophs: use sunlight energy to make own carbon compounds from carbon dioxide
    • chemoautotrophs: use energy from inorganic chemicals to make own carbon compunds from carbon dioxide
