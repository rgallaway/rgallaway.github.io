3.02 Membranes and Transport

Membrane Structure

  • Phospholipid bilayer
    • Hydrophilic phosphate “heads” facing aqueous environment on either side
    • Hydrophobic “tails” in the middle
    • Two “leaflets” have different lipid compositions
  • Membrane also contains an equal part of proteins that cross the membrane (transmembrane proteins) or are embedded in membrane (integral membrane proteins)
    • Peripheral membrane proteins are loosly & reversibly associated
  • Also contain carbohydrates (oligosaccharides) covalently attached to membrane proteins or lipids
  • Lipids & integral membrane proteins diffuse laterally within membrane - “fluid mosaic model”
    • Phospholipids & integral proteins do not spontaneously cross bilayer or flip across it

Membrane Lipids Differ in the 3 Domains of Life

  • Archaea have isoprene chains instead of fatty acid, L-glycerol instead of D-glycerol, ether linkages instead of ester linkages
  • Bacteria & Eukarya have membrane phospholipids with ester-linked fatty acid chains
    • Differences make Archaeal membranes more resistant to extreme heat and pH
  • Eukaryotes have sterols and sphingolipids that aren’t found in Archaea or Bacteria
    • Major components of eukaryotic plasma membranes
    • Sterols are essential, modulate membrane fluidity and permeability
    • Sterols & sphingolipids organize regions of membrane into lipid rafts to organize signaling proteins into functional complexes
      • Lipid raft: microdomain in plasma membrane with reduced fluidity
  • Sterol synthesis requires 11 molecules of oxygen, so could not have evolved before Great Oxygenation Event
    • Coincides with origin of eukaryotes
  • Bacteria have hopanoids, bacterial equivalent of sterols
    • 5 rings, do not require oxygen for synthesis

Cells Regulate Membrane Fluidity by Adjusting Membrane Lipid Composition

  • Fluidity varies with temperature: membranes become more fluid at higher temperatures, rigid at lower temps
    • Must maintain balance to allow movement within membrane without compromising integrity or leakage
  • Sterols buffer fluidity and permeability over broad temp ranges
    • Cholesterol increases membrane packing to reduce fluidity & permeability
  • Fatty acid tails affect fluidity with variations in length & double bonds
    • Saturated fatty acids have no double bonds in hydrocarbon chain, maximum number of hydrogens
    • Unsaturated fatty acids have >= 1 double bond, 2 fewer hydrogens per double bond
  • Cis-unsaturated: remaining hydrogens are on the same side of the molecule, results in bend in the hydrocarbon chain
    • Naturally occurring
  • Trans-unsaturated are rare in nature, but produced when vegetable oils are partially hydrogenated
  • Longer fatty acids are more rigid -> reduce fluidity & permeability
  • Cis-unsaturated increase fluidity & permeability by disrupting close packing of tails.
    • Cis-polyunsaturated are even more bent & disruptive
  • Organisms adapt to cold by increasing cis-unsaturated acids to keep membranes fluid
  • Animal fats with low levels of cis-unsaturated and high levels of saturated are solid at room temperature
  • Vegetable oils with high levels of cis-unsaturated fats are liquid at room temperature

Membrane Permeability and Osmosis

  • Bilayer is semi-permeable: some molecules can rapidly diffuse across, while others only slowly or not at all
  • Small uncharged molecules like O2 & CO2 can freely diffuse, while charged molecules or polar molecules cannot
    • Water diffuses slowly (osmosis)
  • Diffusion results of net movement down concentration gradient

Membrane Proteins and Transport

  • Membranes have dedicated transport proteins with transmembrane domains
    • Form channels in membranes for specific molecules
    • Water transport mediated by aquaporins
  • Facilitated diffusion: movement of molecules through protein channels specific for molecule
    • Net transport down concentration gradient
  • To move against concentration gradient, active transport can be used with expenditure of energy

Kinetics of Transport

  • Facilitated diffusion shows saturation kinetics because of limited numbers of protein channels
  • As concentration difference increases, rate of diffusion increases
    • Protein channels reach limit in flow of molecules, while simple diffusion does not reach a limit
