3.04 Energy and Enzymes

Laws of Thermodynamics

  • First: Energy cannot be created or destroyed.
  • Second: In any conversion, some energy is wasted as heat; entropy of a closed system always increases
    • An economy based on endless growth is Unsustainable
    • Energy pyramids: each trophic level only captures at most 10% of the energy from lower level
    • Work within organisms releases heat, raising overall entropy

Gibbs Free Energy

  • Measure of the amount of work that is potentially obtainable
    • Measure change in free energy: ΔG = ΔH – TΔS
      • H = enthalpy (heat energy content)
      • T = absolute temperature (Kelvin)
      • S = entropy
    • If delta G < 0, reaction is exergonic, releases free energy, progresses spontaneously
    • If delta G > 0, reaction is endergonic, absorbs energy, will progress only if free energy is put into the system

Free Energy and Equilibrium

  • If delta G = 0, reaction is in equilibrium, so no net change
  • Cells couple exergonic reactions to endergonic reactions so that net free energy change is negative

Reaction Rates

  • Sign of delta G determines direction of the reaction, but magnitude does not predict speed
  • Rate is determined by the activation energy barrier
    • Reactions with high activation energy proceed slowly because only a few molecules get enough energy
  • Enzymes speed up reactions by lowering activation energy barrier
    • Act as biological catalysts, most are proteins but some are RNA molecules
    • Only ones with specific shape & chemical organization can interact with amino acid side chains at the active site
  • Enzyme-catalyzed reactions have saturation kinetics
    • Velocity of reactions increases with concentration of substrate
      • At high concentrations, quantity of enzyme molecules is limiting
      • At saturation, further increases in concentration have no effect; only way to speed up reaction is to increase amount of enzyme
    • Kinetic properties of enzymes are defined by Vmax and Km
      • Vmax is maximum rate enzyme can work at saturating concentrations of substrate
      • Km (Michaelis constant) is substrate concentration that produces 1/2 Vmax, affinity of enzyme for substrate

Enzyme Inhibitors

  • Targets of many drugs, many enzymes are regulated by allosteric regulators that bind at different site from active site
  • Noncompetitive inhibitors act allosterically
  • Competitive inhibitors compete with substrate for binding to active site
